03 — Project Understanding

Comprehensive Understanding of WREC's Vision

The Cedar Ridge 230kV Transmission Line and Substation Expansion project represents a critical investment in the long-term reliability and capacity of WREC's transmission network. We understand that this project is driven by growing load demands in the eastern Mesa County service territory, the need to reduce transmission congestion along the existing 115kV corridor, and WREC's strategic objective to provide a second high-voltage feed into the region to enhance N-1 contingency performance in compliance with NERC TPL reliability standards.

Transmission Line Scope

The project scope encompasses the construction of approximately 22 miles of new 230kV single-circuit transmission line extending from the proposed Cedar Ridge Substation site to the existing Plateau Substation northwest of Palisade. The route traverses a mix of BLM-administered public land and private agricultural parcels across terrain that ranges from relatively flat irrigated bottomland along the Colorado River corridor to elevated mesa terrain with rocky substrate, arroyos, and moderate slopes. Based on the route alignment provided in the RFP and our review of publicly available geospatial data, we anticipate the line will require approximately 135 to 145 steel lattice and tubular steel monopole structures, with an average span of approximately 800 to 1,000 feet depending on terrain constraints.

We have identified several key routing considerations that will influence structure placement and construction methodology. The crossing of the Colorado River near mile marker 8.5 will require extended spans utilizing heavy-angle lattice towers with engineered foundations designed for the alluvial soils present in the floodplain. Additionally, the segment between miles 14 and 18, which traverses the Jurassic shale formations on the upper mesa, will likely require drilled shaft foundations with depths of 25 to 40 feet to achieve adequate bearing capacity in the weathered Mancos Shale present in this geology. Our geotechnical subcontractor, CTL Thompson, has extensive experience characterizing these formations throughout the Grand Valley and will perform a full subsurface investigation during the pre-construction phase.

Substation Scope

The new Cedar Ridge Substation will be constructed as a 230/115/69kV facility on an approximately 8-acre site identified in the RFP documentation. The substation scope includes a 230kV breaker-and-a-half bus configuration, two 230/115kV 200MVA power transformers, a 115/69kV 75MVA autotransformer, associated circuit breakers, disconnect switches, instrument transformers, surge arresters, control house, and a full SCADA/EMS integration package compatible with WREC's existing OSI Monarch platform. The breaker-and-a-half bus arrangement provides the operational flexibility and reliability that WREC requires for a facility that will serve as a critical node in the regional transmission network.

The interconnection scope at the existing Plateau Substation includes the installation of a new 230kV line terminal bay, modifications to the existing bus configuration to accommodate the incoming Cedar Ridge circuit, relay and protection upgrades to integrate the new line into the existing SEL-421 and SEL-411L relay scheme, and associated civil work including new foundations, cable trench extensions, and grounding grid expansion. We understand that the Plateau Substation modifications must be performed during scheduled outage windows, and our team has extensive experience planning and executing hot cutover sequences to minimize system impact.

NERC Compliance: All design and construction activities will comply with NERC FAC-001, FAC-002, and TPL-001 through TPL-004 reliability standards, as well as applicable WECC regional standards for the Rocky Mountain region.

04 — Technical Approach

A Proven Methodology for Complex Transmission

Our construction methodology for the Cedar Ridge project is organized into three overlapping phases designed to optimize resource utilization, minimize environmental disturbance, and deliver the project within the 18-month construction window. Each phase incorporates rigorous quality control checkpoints, environmental compliance monitoring, and safety reviews to ensure that the finished product meets or exceeds WREC's technical specifications and all applicable codes and standards.

Phase 1 Pre-Construction & Site Preparation — Months 1–4

Phase 1 begins with a comprehensive pre-construction effort including final geotechnical investigation, topographic survey verification, and detailed construction engineering. Our engineering team will work collaboratively with WREC's design engineer-of-record to finalize structure loading calculations, foundation designs, and conductor sag-tension analyses using PLS-CADD and PLS-POLE/TOWER software. We will develop a complete set of construction-level drawings, including plan and profile sheets, structure assembly details, foundation designs, and grounding plans, all reviewed and stamped by our Colorado-licensed Professional Engineer.

Concurrent with the engineering effort, our field teams will initiate access road construction and improvement along the 22-mile corridor. Mesa County's terrain presents access challenges that require careful planning. We anticipate constructing approximately 12 miles of new temporary access roads and improving an additional 8 miles of existing ranch roads and BLM two-tracks to support construction equipment access. All access road work will comply with the BLM Right-of-Way stipulations and the project's Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan (SWPPP). Temporary erosion control measures, including silt fencing, wattles, and stabilized construction entrances, will be installed prior to any ground disturbance.

Material procurement represents a critical-path activity that will commence immediately upon notice to proceed. Current lead times for 230kV-class steel lattice towers range from 20 to 28 weeks depending on complexity, and high-voltage conductor (we anticipate 1590 kcmil ACSS/TW Lapwing based on the RFP specifications) carries a typical lead time of 16 to 22 weeks. Our procurement team will leverage MYR Group's national purchasing agreements with key suppliers including Valmont Industries (structures), Southwire Company (conductor), and Hubbell Power Systems (hardware and insulators) to secure favorable pricing and priority production slots. All materials will be staged at Sturgeon Electric's Grand Junction yard and transported to designated structure sites via the improved access road network.

Environmental pre-construction activities will include a final biological survey to confirm the absence of threatened or endangered species within the construction corridor, cultural resource monitoring during ground disturbance activities per the project's Programmatic Agreement, and coordination with the Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment for any required air quality permits associated with fugitive dust generation during road construction.

Phase 2 Foundations, Structures & Substation Construction — Months 3–14

Foundation installation will proceed from both ends of the line simultaneously, utilizing two dedicated foundation crews. Each crew will consist of a truck-mounted drill rig (Watson 3500 or equivalent), a support crane (Link-Belt 100-ton hydraulic), concrete transit mixers, rebar setting crews, and quality control inspectors. Drilled shaft foundations in the mesa terrain will be designed for the Mancos Shale bearing conditions identified during the Phase 1 geotechnical investigation, with anticipated shaft diameters of 48 to 72 inches and depths of 25 to 40 feet. In the river bottomland and irrigated agricultural areas where the water table is shallow, we will employ temporary casing and tremie concrete placement methods to ensure foundation integrity.

Steel structure erection will follow approximately four to six weeks behind the foundation crews to allow for concrete curing and foundation acceptance testing. Our erection plan utilizes two lattice tower assembly and erection crews and one monopole erection crew. Lattice towers will be pre-assembled on the ground at each structure location using gin pole and mobile crane erection methods. Monopole structures will be erected using 200-ton class hydraulic cranes staged on improved pads adjacent to each structure location. All structural bolt torquing will be performed and verified in accordance with AISC standards, and each structure will undergo a post-erection survey to confirm plumbness and alignment within specified tolerances.

Substation construction at the Cedar Ridge site will proceed concurrently with transmission line construction. The substation work sequence begins with site grading, drainage installation, and perimeter fencing, followed by below-grade work including cable trench, ground grid installation (using bare copper conductor in a grid pattern with ground rods driven to 25-foot depth), and equipment foundations. We will install a total of approximately 85 drilled pier foundations for major equipment, bus supports, and line dead-end structures. Foundation concrete will be batch-mixed on-site using a portable batch plant to ensure consistent quality and rapid placement.

Above-grade substation construction includes the erection of the bus support structures, installation of the breaker-and-a-half bus configuration, setting of the two 200MVA power transformers (each weighing approximately 350,000 pounds and requiring specialized heavy-haul transport from the manufacturer), circuit breaker installation, disconnect switch installation, and instrument transformer mounting. Our electrical crews will perform all bus connection work, insulator installation, and equipment termination. The control house will be a pre-fabricated, climate-controlled building furnished with relay panels, SCADA interface cabinets, battery systems, and station service equipment, delivered and set in place by crane during month 10.

Phase 3 Wire Stringing, Commissioning & Energization — Months 12–18

Conductor stringing operations represent the most visible and technically demanding phase of transmission line construction. Our stringing plan employs a single, fully equipped pulling and tensioning crew utilizing a 90,000-pound capacity hydraulic puller (TSE International Model 3090) and a multi-drum hydraulic tensioner (TSE Model 3060) to maintain controlled tension throughout the stringing process. The 1590 kcmil ACSS/TW conductor will be strung in a controlled-tension, sequential stringing operation moving from the Cedar Ridge Substation northward to the Plateau Substation. Each stringing setup will encompass approximately 3 to 5 miles of line depending on terrain and angle point locations.

Special stringing procedures will be employed for the Colorado River crossing, Interstate 70 crossing (if applicable), and any railroad or existing power line crossings. These crossings require the installation of temporary guard structures and aerial marker balls, traffic control plans, and coordination with the relevant authorities (CDOT, UPRR, or other utilities). Our crews have performed hundreds of critical crossings throughout Colorado and maintain all required permits and certifications for interstate highway and railroad crossing work.

Following conductor installation, the overhead ground wire (OPGW) will be strung to provide both lightning shielding and fiber optic communications capability along the corridor. Optical time-domain reflectometer (OTDR) testing will be performed on each fiber strand to verify splice quality and end-to-end attenuation, with test results documented and delivered to WREC in the final project closeout package.

System commissioning and energization activities begin in month 16 with point-to-point wiring verification, relay testing, and SCADA integration testing at both the Cedar Ridge and Plateau substations. Our commissioning team, led by a dedicated Commissioning Manager, will execute a comprehensive test plan that includes primary injection testing of all current and voltage transformers, relay functional testing per manufacturer specifications, breaker timing tests, transformer oil analysis and turns-ratio testing, ground grid resistance testing, and a full end-to-end trip test of all protective relay schemes. Upon successful completion of all commissioning tests, we will coordinate with WREC and the applicable Reliability Coordinator to schedule energization during an approved switching sequence.

Daily Safety Protocols

Every work day on the Cedar Ridge project will begin with a mandatory tailboard safety meeting led by the crew foreman and attended by all personnel working on that crew's specific task. The tailboard meeting covers the day's scope of work, identification of specific hazards associated with that day's activities, review of the applicable Job Safety Analysis (JSA), confirmation of required personal protective equipment, emergency action plan review, and an open discussion period where any crew member can raise concerns or suggest safer work methods. No work begins until every crew member has signed the tailboard form acknowledging their understanding of the day's hazards and controls.

In addition to daily tailboard meetings, our Safety Director, James Caldwell, will conduct weekly project-wide safety meetings every Monday morning that address trending safety topics, review any near-miss reports from the previous week, present toolbox talks on relevant seasonal hazards (heat stress, cold weather working, high-wind procedures), and recognize crews or individuals who have demonstrated exceptional safety leadership. These weekly meetings also serve as a forum for reviewing and updating the project-specific safety plan based on evolving site conditions.

Site-Specific Safety Plan

Prior to mobilization, our safety team will develop a comprehensive site-specific safety plan for the Cedar Ridge project that addresses the unique hazards associated with this corridor, including but not limited to: working at heights during structure erection and wire stringing; heavy equipment operation on mesa terrain with limited access and steep grades; exposure to Mancos Shale dust (which may contain naturally occurring asbestos in some formations, requiring air monitoring); electrical hazards during the Plateau Substation interconnection work; wildlife encounters including rattlesnakes common to the mesa terrain; and extreme weather conditions including high winds, lightning, and temperature extremes typical of western Colorado.
